Vortex-induced vibrations

My PhD project consisted of several sub-projects. These are some of them.

Field test on custom chimney

This work was done by designing, constructing and executing a field experiment on a 35.5 m tall industrial chimney with a tip diameter of 2 m. This chimney was placed in a monitored field and the wind speed near it and the acceleration of chimney was measured. The main finding was that the displacement was dependent on the incoming atmospheric boundary layer which varied with direction and had turbulence intensities much lower than the design codes suggested

See preprint and results here

2D wind tunnel tests at super-critical Reynolds numbers

This work was done by measuring the unsteady pressure around a cylinder and the wake characteristics behind it. The tests revealed some new knowledge on the shedding frequency behind large cylinders that we hope to publish soon. In addition, the work made it possible to determine the best surface roughness for simulating super-critical Reynolds numbers (>106) at lower ones. This was done using regularly attached surface ribs with a rectangular cross-section.

Results to be published soon

3D response of cylinders at super-critical Reynolds numbers

In addition to determining the aerodynamics around a cylinder, we found ways to simulate the response of chimneys at super-critical Reynolds numbers (>106) using roughness and a turbulent atmospheric boundary layer. These tests revealed that the boundary layer was an important factor when simulating the response. By combining these tests with predictive models, the best model for a given situation was found.

Results to be published soon

Predicting the response due to vortex-induced vibrations

The current design models used in the Eurocode either cannot predict amplitude responses or fails to predict them when the Scruton number (a mass-damping parameter) is low. An attempt to fix both of these problems was done by deriving an analytical expression for the response due to vortex-induced vibrations. This was done by approximating an existing nonlinear wake oscillator (by Facchinetti et al.) using the method of averaging. The approximated model matched well with numerical integrations and the response of cylinders with low Scruton numbers. With this model, an equation for the lock-in region could be derived and its changes with mass and damping was explored.

Two-stage high temperature hybrid heat pump with parallel heat sinks

This work was done during an internship with Sintef Energy. The aim of this work was to determine if a single high temperature heat pump could supply enough heat for two sinks at different temperatures. We achieved this but needed to put in extra heat exchangers/
